Public health issues often present a spatial problem that requires assertion of control over private spaces and individuals. The nature of communicable disease is that it spreads through direct contact or bodily secretions, and thus the first victims of epidemics are often populations that live in crowded, impoverished, and unsanitary conditions. In fulfilling their mission, public health officials inevitably encounter people who are at least partially defined by their social identities: minorities, immigrants, and the poor. The subsequent language of differentiating the risk groups for disease can overlap, or even embrace, racialized assumptions about these groups that encourage political and social discrimination. Michael Willrich’s Pox and Natalia Molina’s Fit to Be Citizens examine these sort of public health encounters from a social history and cultural studies perspective, respectively.

Willrich tackles racialization of public health as a by-product of limited knowledge. Scientific understanding of smallpox transmission at the time was that contagion through fomites (contaminated objects) was more virulent than face-to-face respiratory transmission, which created perception of smallpox as a “filth” disease. While fomite infection does occur, this “long-standing association of smallpox with the filthy poor was grounded more in class and racial bias than in (today’s) medical reality.” [Willrich, 349] Crowded living conditions of the poor presented the danger, less so the clutter within. Public health agents were concentrating on the right locations for the (later-discovered) wrong reasons.

Public health officials casually expressed, and espoused, the prevailing racist and class sentiments of their day; repellent as those views are to the contemporary reader, Willrich assesses the operational response to the smallpox outbreaks to have been based on sound science of the time:

To dismiss official accounts out of hand – or to read them only as elite ideology – is to forgo all hope of recovering the social experience of disease. The wonderfully idiosyncratic epistolary form that public health reports took in the era inspires at least some confidence in their contents….Even assuming broad agreement regarding matters of race and class, it would have taken a racial conspiracy of an implausible scale to make all of these reports tell a common story of the epidemic’s prevalence among African Americans and poor whites, if there were not some basis for this in fact… Public health officials made a revealing leap, however, when they concluded that (the victims) were morally culpable for the spread of smallpox. [Willrich, 98]

In Fit to Be Citizens, epidemiology as a subject is subordinated to a closer examination of the biases of public officials and their political weaponization of medical data. Molina’s approach to the question of racialization is a deep dive into the narrow geography and fragmented, nigh inscrutable, archives of Los Angeles County, California. Her study is important because it moves outside the familiar black-white binary that dominates racial discourse in the United States; Los Angeles County had a miniscule African American population during this period, while Mexicans, Chinese, Japanese, and poor whites competed for position within a racial hierarchy. [Molina, 6-7] The extent to which the “sound science” of the time accepted and perpetuated racial stereotypes is illustrated by official documents depicting the Chinese as disease carriers (not the conditions in which they lived), and asserting that Mexicans “shunned water, …typhus was uniquely Mexican,” and Japanese birth rates were a “yellow peril.” [Molina, pp. 26, 69, 91]

Defining Race: Race identity in the United States is a social construct that typically places “whiteness” as the standard against which other racial identities are defined. However, Eric Goldstein observes in The Price of Whiteness: Jews, Race, and American Identity that the “…black-white racial dichotomy has functioned in American history less as an accurate description of social reality than as an ideology.”[Goldstein, 3] Instead of this binary, whiteness was understood in practice as a continuum of racial groups who could make increasing claims on whiteness and thus candidacy for full inclusion in American culture. Goldstein disagrees with scholars who “posit a fairly uncomplicated embrace of whiteness by immigrant groups… (and that) such ambiguity was quickly resolved as immigrants responded to overwhelming dominance of the black-white paradigm.” [Goldstein, 4] Molina also rejects this binary, at least insofar as the population of Los Angeles, CA during her period of study. She writes of the “…notion of race as a graded continuum shading from white, at the top, downward through various forms of “nonwhite,” represented by the city’s Chinese, Japanese, and Mexican populations. [Molina, 6] She subscribes to the construct described by Claire Jean Kim in Bitter Fruit: The Politics of Black-Korean Conflict in New York City that thinks of racial ordering

…not as a single-scale hierarchy (A over B over C) but as a field structured by at least two axes: that of superior/inferior and that of insider/foreigner. Blacks and Whites constitute the major anchors (bottom and top, respectively) of this order, and incoming immigrants and other groups get positioned relative to these two loci.” [Kim, 10]
Kim’s construct is intriguing, but difficult to visualize across a corpus. Texts that discuss racial constructs usually do not stop to say, for example, “A is superior to B, except when C is present. D and B are similarly superior, but B is considered more foreign.” A typical example from an early 20th century legal treatise illustrates the challenge of fleshing out Kim’s racial axes:
Jews, especially Russian Jews, often become frantic with terror after an accident and develop many secondary nervous phenomena. Negroes suffer considerably from fear, being usually very emotional. Warbasse (2) reminds us that the negro, as seen in temperate climates, is less resistant to disease and injury than are the whites, and the death-rate among them is higher at all ages. The same author cautions us to bear in mind that the skull of the African negro is much thicker than that of the white. He says that mulattoes or mixed breeds present the weaknesses of both races and more deficient resistance than either. Warbasse (3) also states that the Jewish people bear surgical operations well; and that injuries and operations are borne particularly well by the people who live exclusively, or nearly so, on a vegetable diet-the Chinese, Japanese, Indians, Mohammedans, and the peasantry of Europe. [Document_id:20004268501, Legal Medicine and Toxicology, 1926]

Keyword search for racial ordering: One approach to this challenge would be to manually search a set of documents for key words and build some sort of table that counts instances of use, perhaps qualifying them with a grading scheme for the context in which they were applied. Such a system has several vulnerabilities. First, the search terms would have to be defined by the user and rely upon serendipitous discovery [Putnam, 386-387] to add forgotten or unfamiliar terms. Second, manually assessing racial ordering against a growing number of contexts would be subject to the biases of the person doing the grading and would be subject to an increasing rate of error. As Laura Putnam warns, “….historians tend to attribute causality to what we see, without formal modeling to act as a brake on our tendency to do so.” [Putnam, 390] Operations research methods could assist with this interpretation, but the database upon which such methods rely would still be vulnerable to these limitations.

A digital history approach: This paper examines how racial ordering may reveal itself through distant reading techniques applied to a corpus of legal treatises. The source for this test is the Making of Modern Law database which contains 22,000 US and British legal treatises published from 1800-1926. [FN database] A subset of 123 medical jurisprudence texts was selected as a corpus that shared similar intellectual thought and resources with public health officials.

A note on racial language in this essay: the language of racial identity during the period under study is minutely detailed, contains discredited science, and is often deeply offensive in today’s society. The use of such taxonomies in this paper reflect racial perceptions as expressed by the texts themselves and do not represent the personal beliefs of the author. The terms used in external analysis of those taxonomies adopt the usage expressed by Goldstein, Kim, Molina, and Willrich.

The strategy for revealing racial ordering involves three steps: 1) creation of racial taxonomies, 2) measurement of racial identity according to those taxonomies, 3) creation of a comparison scales to replicate Kim’s construct.

Step 1: Building Taxonomies of Racial Terms:

The corpus of medical jurisprudence .txt files were read into R and processed using the wordVectors package to create a word embedded model (WEM). [FN - Schmidt] Word embedded models measure spatial relationships between words, which enables examination of these relationships on the basis of their nearness (similarity) or distance (less similarity.)

An advantage of a WEM is that the analyst does not have to rely on a trial and error process of keyword searches to identify a set of related terms. Keyword searches are at risk of being incomplete, inaccurate because of changes in usage, or biases by contemporary sensibilities. In the WEM, the terms are suggested by spatial relationships between the words themselves.By iteratively modeling terms “nearest_to” a set of general “seed” labels (“race”, “races”, “racial”), an inclusive list of overall racial terms was created. This set was used to build the seed terms for individual racial groups and further modeling. As new terms were identified for a specific group, they were added back into the overall racial terms list.

The modeling process required some decisions about inclusion of certain types of words. When modeling for racial terms, the model quickly begins to include religion and geographic locations. For example, “Jew” refers to both race and religion; I included it in the list because the Jewish people are often treated as a race during this period. More generic terms, such as “ethnic” are included because they typically refer to people viewed as “other.” Geographic terms were excluded if they could not also apply to a group of people, i.e., “germany” was excuded, but “germanic” was included. Lastly, anthropological terms such as “australoid” and “mongoloid” were excluded from the term sets, although they reappear in the nearest_to modeling.

Once the term sets were identified, they were modeled to identify the 150 words closest to that vector set. The result is a set of racial taxonomies derived from the documents themselves:

Having created taxonomies and vector sets for each racial group, the next step was to create a pair of scales that aligned with Kim’s construct: “…a field structured by at least two axes: that of superior/inferior and that of insider/foreigner. Blacks and Whites constitute the major anchors (bottom and top, respectively) of this order, and incoming immigrants and other groups get positioned relative to these two loci.” [Kim, 10] The black/white scale was easy enough to create, but the insider/foreigner scale required a little more thought. After some trial and error, the scale was created by subtracting the all_races vector from the white vector. After testing the scale on three examples, the scale appears to be stable. Graphing racial terms against this pair of scales reveals racial ordering as it exists within the corpus:

Where are the Irish?</>

The term “irish” did not arise during any of the modeling. This is surprising, given their large scale immigration to the United States during this period and their history as “less white” than other groups. A model of the term explains why: “irish” does appear in the texts, but not in significant relation to other racial terms at this scale. When the subset of medical jurisprudence texts after 1899 was modeled, “irish” appeared as a European term.
